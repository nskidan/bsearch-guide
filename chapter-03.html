Chapter 3

How Search Actually Works

From keyword matching to AI-powered understanding — how BSearch finds the right document out of 550,000 in under two seconds.

This chapter explains every concept behind BSearch’s search pipeline, from the simplest (exact word matching) to the most sophisticated (AI reranking and confidence gating). No code — just the ideas and the reasoning. If you understand this chapter, everything else in the guide will click into place.

3.1 BM25 Keyword Search

BM25 (Best Match 25) — The industry-standard algorithm for keyword search. It scores how well a document matches a set of search words by looking at how often those words appear in the document and how rare they are across all documents.

Keyword search is the most intuitive form of search: find documents that contain the exact words the user typed. BM25 improves on raw word-matching by considering two factors:

  1. Term frequency — how often the search term appears in the document. More mentions means more relevant, up to a point of diminishing returns.
  2. Inverse document frequency — how rare the search term is across all documents. A match on a rare word like “Catalyst” is worth far more than a match on a common word like “the.”
IB Analogy

Keyword search is like searching by exact deal name. If you type “Acme Corp CIM”, BM25 finds every document that contains those exact words and ranks them by relevance. Fast, precise, predictable — like looking up a folder by the label on its tab.

BM25 works beautifully for specific names, ticker symbols, and deal codes. But it has a fundamental limitation: it only matches on exact words, not on meaning. If you search for “what multiple did we use” but the document says “EV/EBITDA”, keyword search will miss it entirely. Those are the same concept expressed in different words, and BM25 has no idea they are related.

3.2 Vector Embeddings

Embedding — A list of numbers (in BSearch’s case, 1,024 numbers) that represents the meaning of a piece of text. Think of embeddings as mathematical fingerprints of meaning — two pieces of text about the same concept produce similar fingerprints, even if they use completely different words.

Vector — A list of numbers representing a point in space. A GPS coordinate is a 2-number vector (latitude, longitude). An embedding is a 1,024-number vector that pinpoints a text’s position in “meaning space.”

This is the concept that makes modern search dramatically better than keyword matching alone. An AI model (BSearch uses Voyage AI’s voyage-context-3) reads a piece of text and converts it into a list of 1,024 numbers. These numbers are not random — they are coordinates in a vast mathematical space where texts about similar topics end up near each other.

IB Analogy — Searching by Meaning

Keyword search is like searching a filing cabinet by the exact label on each folder. Vector search is like having an analyst who understands what you are looking for. When you ask for “what multiple”, the analyst knows to pull documents that discuss “EV/EBITDA”, “trading comps”, and “valuation multiples” — because they all mean similar things, even though the words are different.

When you search, your query also gets converted into a 1,024-number vector. The system then finds documents whose vectors are closest to your query’s vector — meaning the documents whose meaning is closest to what you asked for. Closeness is measured using cosine similarity, which compares the angle between two vectors. Smaller angle means more similar meaning.

In the diagram above, “what multiple did we use” and “EV/EBITDA of 8.5x” produce similar vectors (their numbers are close), even though the words are completely different. “Office lease agreement” produces a very different vector because it is about a different topic entirely. The embedding model understands the conceptual relationship that keyword search cannot see.

Contextual Embeddings

BSearch uses contextual embeddings, meaning each chunk of a document is processed with awareness of the full document it came from. A chunk that says “Revenue was $45M in FY2024” in isolation is ambiguous — which company? But the model knows this chunk came from the Catalyst CIM for a home health company, so its embedding captures that full context. This is covered in detail in Chapter 4.

3.3 Hybrid Search

Hybrid Search — Running keyword search (BM25) and vector search simultaneously on the same query, then merging the results. You get the precision of exact word matching and the intelligence of semantic understanding.

BSearch does not choose between keyword search and vector search. It runs both at the same time and combines the results. Here is why that matters:

Keyword Search (BM25) excels at…

  • Finding exact names (“Project Falcon”)
  • Matching ticker symbols (“AAPL”)
  • Locating specific deal codes
  • Speed — very fast lookups

Vector Search excels at…

  • Understanding synonyms and related concepts
  • Matching “trading multiple” to “EV/EBITDA”
  • Handling natural-language questions
  • Finding conceptually similar documents

Neither one is perfect on its own. Keyword search misses conceptual matches; vector search can sometimes miss exact names because it focuses on meaning rather than specific words. By running both and merging the results, BSearch catches what either method alone would miss.

IB Analogy

Think of it like sourcing a deal. You run two searches in parallel: one analyst checks your proprietary database for exact company name matches, while another scans industry reports for companies with similar business models. You then combine both lists and remove duplicates. Hybrid search does exactly this — two different approaches, merged into one candidate set.

3.4 Reranking

Reranking — A second-pass AI model that takes the initial search results and re-scores them to find the truly best matches. BSearch uses Cohere’s reranker, which reads each candidate result alongside the query and assigns a new relevance score from 0 to 1.

The hybrid search step returns the top 20 candidate results. But “top 20” from a fast, broad search is not the same as “the 5 best answers.” The initial search optimizes for speed and recall (not missing anything relevant). Reranking optimizes for precision (finding the most relevant).

Here is how it works: the Cohere reranker reads each of the 20 candidates alongside the original query and assigns a new relevance score. This model is slower than the initial search — it reads every word carefully, not just comparing vectors — but it only has to evaluate 20 documents instead of 550,000, so it is still fast (~200–400ms).

IB Analogy

A junior analyst does a first pass and pulls 20 comparable companies from CapIQ. They cast a wide net — any company in the right sector, right size range. Then a senior analyst reviews the pile — reads each company profile against the original criteria and narrows the list to the 5 that truly fit: right business model, right growth profile, right transaction type. The junior analyst is the hybrid search. The senior analyst is the reranker.

The Reranker Uses the Original Query

BSearch expands your query with synonyms and alternative phrasings to help the initial keyword search cast a wider net (for example, expanding “multiple” to include “EV/EBITDA”). But the reranker always scores against your original query, not the expanded version. This is intentional — expansion helps find candidates, but the reranker judges relevance against what you actually asked. This prevents expansion noise from inflating scores of marginally relevant results.

Per-Chunk Floor

Before the reranker’s output goes any further, BSearch applies a per-chunk floor of 0.10. Any individual chunk that scores below 0.10 from the reranker is discarded entirely — it is so weakly related to the query that including it would add noise rather than value. Only chunks that clear this floor are considered for the confidence tiers described in the next section.

3.5 Relevance Gating

Relevance Gating — A system that evaluates the reranker’s confidence scores and decides how to respond: confidently, cautiously, or not at all. It prevents the system from generating plausible-sounding answers when it has not actually found relevant documents.

This is one of the most important features in BSearch. In investment banking, a wrong answer presented with confidence is far more dangerous than an honest “I don’t know.” Relevance gating ensures the system communicates its confidence level honestly.

After the reranker scores each result (and chunks below the 0.10 floor have been discarded), BSearch classifies the overall result quality into one of three confidence tiers:

Tier Rerank Score System Behavior
HIGH ≥ 0.50 Confident answer. Full synthesis from the retrieved documents — direct response with citations.
LOW 0.15 – 0.50 Hedged answer. The system responds but includes a disclaimer: the retrieved documents may not fully answer the query. The user sees a yellow confidence banner.
NONE < 0.15 No relevant results. The system returns a “no relevant results found” message rather than attempting to synthesize an answer from irrelevant documents.
IB Analogy

Think of how a good analyst responds to requests. If they find the exact answer, they give it with confidence. If they find something related but are not sure it fully answers the question, they flag it: “I found this, but it may not be exactly what you’re looking for.” And if they searched everywhere and found nothing relevant, they say so honestly rather than making something up. Relevance gating gives BSearch the same three-level response discipline.

Initial Thresholds, Not Final

The specific score thresholds (0.50, 0.15, 0.10) are initial defaults based on typical Cohere rerank score distributions. They will be calibrated against BSearch’s actual corpus during acceptance testing — running 30+ queries across all types, verifying which scores correlate with genuinely relevant vs. irrelevant results, and adjusting accordingly.

3.6 Recency Boosting

Recency Boosting — Giving newer documents a higher search score. BSearch applies score multipliers based on when a document was last modified, so that more recent documents rank higher when content relevance is otherwise similar.

In investment banking, the most recent version of a document is almost always the one you want. If there are five drafts of a purchase agreement, you want the latest one. If two CIMs exist for the same company, the newer one reflects current financials. Stale valuations surfacing as top results destroy trust in the system.

BSearch applies two tiers of recency boosting, implemented as score multipliers in the Azure AI Search scoring profile:

Document Age Score Multiplier Rationale
Modified within the last 30 days 1.5× Active deals typically have a 2–4 week document update cycle. This captures the “current deal” window.
Modified within the last 90 days 1.2× Captures recently completed deals and documents that are still operationally relevant.
Older than 90 days 1.0× (no boost) Older documents still appear if their content is highly relevant — the boost is a tiebreaker, not an override.

The boost is a multiplier, not an override. A highly relevant old document will still beat a barely relevant new one. But when two documents are equally relevant based on content, the more recent one will rank higher. This matches how bankers naturally think about document priority.

3.7 The Full Pipeline: From Query to Answer

Now that you understand each component, here is how they all work together when you type a query into BSearch:

    P1 --> CL["Classify the query\n(Regex ~5ms or Haiku ~150ms)"]
    P1 --> EM["Generate query embedding\n(Voyage AI ~100-300ms)"]
    CL --> S["Azure AI Search\nHybrid: BM25 + Vector + Recency"]
    EM --> S

  1. Classification — the system figures out what kind of query you asked (file lookup, data extraction, or multi-document synthesis). Simple filename searches are identified instantly by regex; everything else goes to Haiku for classification.
  2. Embedding — your query is converted into a 1,024-number meaning vector, running in parallel with classification.
  3. Hybrid search — BM25 keyword search and vector search both query the index simultaneously, with recency boosting applied. The results are merged into a candidate set of ~20 chunks.
  4. Reranking — Cohere’s reranker reads each of the 20 candidates against your original query and re-scores them.
  5. Per-chunk floor — any chunk scoring below 0.10 is discarded.
  6. Relevance gating — the system evaluates overall confidence (HIGH / LOW / NONE) and decides how to respond.
  7. Synthesis — Claude reads the top-ranked chunks and streams an answer back to you, with the appropriate confidence framing.

3.8 The Latency Budget

Speed matters. A search tool that takes 10 seconds to respond will not get used. BSearch targets a time-to-first-token (the moment you start seeing the answer stream in) of under 3 seconds for standard queries. Here is where the time actually goes:

Stage Typical (p50) Worst Case (p99) Notes
Classification ~5ms ~5ms Regex fast path for file lookups
Classification ~150ms ~250ms Haiku (all other queries), runs in parallel with embedding
Query Embedding ~150ms ~400ms+ Voyage AI, runs in parallel with Haiku; 500ms timeout
Parallel Critical Path ~150ms ~400ms Whichever of Haiku or embedding finishes last
Azure AI Search ~100ms ~200ms Hybrid query (BM25 + vector + recency), sequential
Cohere Reranking ~200ms ~400ms Re-scores top 20 candidates, sequential
Claude Synthesis ~500ms ~1s Time to first token; answer then streams incrementally
Total (time to first token) ~950ms ~1.75s Well within the 3-second target

Several things to notice in this budget:

  • Parallelism is the key lever. Classification and embedding run at the same time, so you pay for the slower of the two (~150ms), not the sum (~300ms). This parallelization saves 100–300ms on every query.
  • The reranker is the biggest sequential cost at 200–400ms, but it is also the biggest quality lever. Removing it to save time would degrade result quality far more than the time savings are worth.
  • Synthesis streams. The ~500ms–1s figure is time to first token. You start seeing the answer immediately, then it fills in over the next 1–3 seconds. This makes the experience feel faster than the total wall-clock time.
  • File lookups are faster. When the regex fast path identifies a file lookup query, the system skips both Haiku classification and embedding entirely, bringing total latency down to ~600–900ms.
Why This Budget Has Margin

Even at p99 (the worst 1% of queries), the total is ~1.75 seconds — well under the 3-second target. This margin is intentional. It absorbs real-world variability: API rate limiting, network jitter, cold caches, and the occasional Voyage AI latency spike. If the embedding call times out (500ms hard limit), the system falls back to BM25-only search rather than making the user wait.

Key Takeaway

BSearch does not rely on any single search technique. It layers six approaches — keyword matching, semantic vectors, recency boosting, AI reranking, per-chunk filtering, and confidence gating — so that each method covers the others’ blind spots. The result: whether you search by exact deal name, by concept, or by natural-language question, the right documents surface — and the system tells you honestly how confident it is in the results.
