Chapter 1

What BSearch Does

The problem, the product, and what it means for how Berenson works.

1.1 Executive Summary

BSearch is an enterprise search and AI synthesis system built for Berenson & Company. It connects to the firm's Microsoft 365 tenant — roughly 550,000 documents across SharePoint and OneDrive — and gives all 18 users the ability to ask questions in plain English and get real answers, not just a list of file links.

The system reads every Word document, Excel model, PowerPoint deck, and PDF in the firm's file structure. When you search, it finds the relevant content, reads it, and streams back a written answer with source citations — typically in under three seconds. For questions that span multiple deals or documents, it takes a few seconds longer but synthesizes information that would otherwise require hours of manual digging.

The One-Sentence Version

BSearch turns Berenson's 550,000 documents into a system that answers questions — not one that returns file links you have to open and read yourself.

1.2 The Junior Analyst Who Never Forgets

IB Analogy

Think of BSearch as a junior analyst who has read every document in every deal folder, remembers all of it, and can answer any question about any deal in under five seconds — without ever pulling up the wrong version. Ask for a specific file, and you get the link. Ask a question about a deal, and you get a written answer with citations. Ask about the full pipeline, and you get a deal-by-deal overview drawn from hundreds of documents.

That analogy is useful because it captures the core design goal: BSearch does not just find files. It reads them, understands them, and gives you the information you were actually looking for. The product is the answer, not the search result.

1.3 The Problem BSearch Solves

Berenson has roughly 550,000 documents spread across SharePoint and OneDrive: Word documents, Excel models, PowerPoint decks, and PDFs. These files contain the collective knowledge of the firm — deal terms, financial metrics, client communications, precedent transactions, and model outputs. But today, finding the right information is painful:

  • Wrong versions waste real time. When someone grabs an outdated model or an old draft of a CIM, the rework can cost 10 or more hours. In a firm of 18 people, that is a significant hit.
  • No precedent search. There is no practical way to ask "What comparable deals have we done?" and get a useful answer. Institutional knowledge lives in people's heads or is buried in folder structures that only make sense if you already know what you are looking for.
  • Universal pain. This is not a problem limited to one team or one role. All 18 people at the firm — from analysts to managing directors — regularly need to find documents or extract information from them.
IB Analogy

Imagine you are staffed on a new healthcare deal and need to reference the fee structure from a comparable transaction Berenson advised on two years ago. Today, you would ask around, dig through folders, and hope you find the right version. With BSearch, you type your question, and the answer appears in seconds — with a link to the source document.

1.4 What You Can Actually Ask

The best way to understand what BSearch does is to see the kinds of questions it can answer. These five queries are the acceptance tests for the system — if BSearch can answer all five well, it is working as designed:

# Query What BSearch Returns What This Tests
1 "Where's the latest GovTech deck?" The correct file with a direct SharePoint link — not three old versions. File lookup: finding the right document, fast.
2 "Which firms did we reach out to for Govini?" A list of firm names, extracted from the relevant documents, with source citations. Data extraction: pulling specific facts from documents.
3 "Find me a good situation overview slide we've put together in the past for a banking deal" A specific slide from a specific deck, with a link to the full presentation. Slide-level retrieval: finding content inside documents, not just the documents themselves.
4 "What multiple did we use for [deal name]?" The EV/EBITDA or other relevant multiple, pulled from the model or deal summary. Synonym expansion: understanding that "multiple" means "EV/EBITDA" in IB context.
5 "Where do we stand on our active deals?" A deal-by-deal overview, organized by deal name, with citations per claim and gaps flagged. Multi-document synthesis: pulling together information from dozens of documents across multiple deals into one coherent answer.

These range from simple (find a file) to complex (synthesize the state of the entire deal pipeline from hundreds of documents). Query #5 is the hardest — it requires BSearch to reason across deals, organize information coherently, and tell you what it does not know as well as what it does.

1.5 The Key Insight: AI Synthesis Is the Product

BSearch is not just a better file search. The core insight behind the system is that AI synthesis is the product. Finding the right document is necessary but not sufficient — what users actually want is the answer that lives inside those documents.

Traditional File Search

You search, get a list of files, open each one, scan for the answer, and hope you picked the right version.

BSearch

You ask a question, BSearch reads the relevant documents, and streams back a written answer with citations and source links.

This distinction matters because it shapes every design decision in the system. The ingestion pipeline does not just index file names and metadata — it parses document content, understands table structures, extracts deal-level entities, and creates the kind of rich, searchable representation that an AI model needs to produce accurate answers.

AI synthesis — The process of an AI model reading multiple pieces of retrieved content and composing a coherent, cited answer. In BSearch, this is handled by Anthropic's Claude Sonnet 4.6, which reads the top search results and writes the response that the user sees.

1.6 Deal Summaries: Pre-Digested Intelligence

One of BSearch's most important features is something the user never directly sees: deal summaries. For every deal with enough documents, BSearch uses a fast AI model (Claude Haiku 4.5) to generate a concise summary of that deal — roughly 400 words covering the deal status, target company, financials, advisors, key contacts, and stage progression.

Deal summary — An AI-generated intelligence brief about a deal, created by reading all of the deal's documents and entity data. Stored as a searchable chunk alongside the deal's actual documents. Automatically refreshed when any of the deal's files change.

These summaries are what make query #5 ("Where do we stand on our active deals?") possible. Without them, answering a pipeline question would require Claude to read and reason across hundreds of raw document chunks from dozens of deals in a single query — too much content, too little structure. With deal summaries, Claude gets pre-digested deal cards that already contain the key facts, making cross-deal reasoning practical and fast.

IB Analogy

Deal summaries are like the one-page deal overviews that a team maintains for each active engagement — except BSearch generates them automatically from every document in the deal folder, updates them whenever anything changes, and makes them instantly available to anyone who searches.

1.7 The User Experience, End to End

From the user's perspective, BSearch is simple: you open the web app, type a question, and get an answer. But behind the scenes, two major pipelines make this possible.

The Ingestion Pipeline (runs in the background)

Before anyone can search, BSearch needs to read and index every document in the firm's SharePoint and OneDrive. This happens automatically:

  1. BSearch connects to Microsoft 365 via the Graph API (Microsoft's official interface for accessing M365 data).
  2. Each document is parsed by a format-aware parser — Word, Excel, PowerPoint, and PDF each get specialized treatment to preserve structure (tables stay as tables, slides stay as slides).
  3. The content is split into searchable chunks, converted into mathematical representations called embeddings (numbers that capture meaning), and stored in Azure AI Search.
  4. Deal-level entities (company names, deal stages, financial metrics) are extracted and stored in a knowledge graph for richer query understanding.
  5. For each deal with enough documents, Haiku 4.5 generates a deal summary — a pre-digested intelligence brief that makes pipeline-level queries possible.
  6. When files change, BSearch detects the update via webhooks (real-time notifications from Microsoft) and re-indexes only what changed — including refreshing the relevant deal summary.

The Query Pipeline (runs when you search)

  1. You type a question in the web app.
  2. BSearch classifies your query: Are you looking for a specific file? Extracting a data point? Or asking a question that spans multiple documents?
  3. It searches the index using a hybrid approach (both keyword matching and semantic meaning).
  4. Results are re-ranked for relevance by a specialized model.
  5. The top results — including any relevant deal summaries — are sent to Claude Sonnet 4.6, which reads them and streams back a written answer with citations.
IB Analogy

The ingestion pipeline is like the first week on a new deal — you read every document in the data room and build a mental model of what is where. The query pipeline is like getting a question from an MD at 11pm: you already know the material, so you can pull the answer immediately and cite your sources.

1.8 Key Constraints and Design Parameters

Every system operates within constraints. These are the ones that shape BSearch's design:

Constraint Value Why It Matters
Total users ~18 Small user base means we can optimize for quality over massive scale. Costs stay low.
Total documents ~550,000 Large enough to require proper indexing and chunking — you cannot brute-force search this.
Latency: file lookup / data extraction <3 seconds to first token If the system feels slow, nobody will use it. Speed is an adoption requirement, not a nice-to-have.
Latency: multi-doc synthesis <5 seconds to first token Cross-document reasoning takes longer, but users get a visual indicator that the system is working.
Content sources (v1) SharePoint / OneDrive only v1 focuses on files. Email (Outlook), Teams bot, and document vision are deferred to later versions.
File types Word, Excel, PowerPoint, PDF Each format gets a specialized parser to preserve structure (especially tables and slides).
Permissions model 5 top-level folders, no sub-file permissions Simplifies access control — BSearch filters results by which folders a user can access.
AI models Claude Sonnet 4.6 (answers), Haiku 4.5 (classification + deal summaries) Sonnet handles the user-facing synthesis. Haiku handles fast, cheap background tasks.

Time to first token (TTFT) — How long the user waits before the first word of the AI response appears on screen. BSearch streams answers as they are generated, so even though the full response takes longer, the user sees progress almost immediately.

1.9 v1 Scope: What's In and What's Not

BSearch is being built incrementally. The first version (v1) focuses on the highest-value, most-tractable features:

In Scope (v1)

  • SharePoint and OneDrive file search
  • Format-aware parsing (Word, Excel, PPT, PDF)
  • Hybrid search (keyword + semantic)
  • AI-synthesized answers via Claude Sonnet 4.6
  • Deal and entity extraction (knowledge graph)
  • Deal summary chunks (Haiku-generated intelligence per deal)
  • Near-real-time sync via webhooks
  • Web app with Azure AD authentication
  • Near-duplicate file detection

Deferred (v2+)

  • Outlook email search
  • Teams bot interface
  • Document vision (images, charts within docs)
  • Excel model summaries (auto-generated model descriptions)
  • Two-pass extraction (Haiku screening then Sonnet deep-read)
  • Sibling chunk retrieval (pulling nearby chunks for context)

1.10 What It Costs

BSearch has two cost components: a one-time ingestion cost to process all 550,000 documents, and an ongoing monthly cost to keep the system running.

Category Cost What It Covers
Monthly ongoing ~$395–510/month Search infrastructure, AI model usage (50–200 queries/day), database, hosting, and monitoring.
One-time ingestion ~$11,000–12,000 Processing all 550K documents: PDF parsing, AI extraction, embedding generation. This is a one-time cost — ongoing updates are incremental and covered by the monthly budget.

The monthly cost is modest because the user base is small (18 people) and the system is designed to minimize AI model calls — one Claude call per query, with cheaper Haiku handling classification and background tasks.

1.11 Why This Matters

BSearch exists because Berenson's collective knowledge — hundreds of thousands of documents spanning years of deals — is only valuable if people can actually access it. Today, finding the right document (or the right version of the right document) is unreliable and slow. BSearch changes that by making every document searchable and every answer extractable, in seconds.

Key Takeaway

BSearch is not just a search engine. It is an AI system that reads your firm's documents and gives you answers. It knows what deal you are asking about, understands IB terminology, generates pre-digested deal intelligence, and returns the information you were actually looking for — cited back to its source, in under five seconds.
